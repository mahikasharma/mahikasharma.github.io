My Projects & Experience

A mix of research, engineering, and things I built just because I was curious.

Data Engineering @ Purple Carrot

During my co-op at Purple Carrot, I built and maintained Python-based data pipelines that powered internal analytics. A major part of the role involved migrating legacy data systems to Snowflake, which required careful schema design and data validation at every step. I also built Looker dashboards that gave the business team visibility into metrics they'd never had before. It was my first real taste of how data engineering sits at the core of every product decision a company makes — and how much craft goes into making pipelines reliable, scalable, and actually usable by non-engineers. The experience taught me a lot about documentation, stakeholder communication, and writing code that your future self won't hate.

Scientific Research @ Academy of Sciences, Czech Republic

I spent time doing research at the Academy of Sciences of the Czech Republic, where I worked on building data pipelines for scientific datasets. Working in an international research environment was eye-opening — the pace is different from industry, the questions are longer-term, and the datasets are often messier and more interesting. I got to apply a lot of the data engineering skills I'd built in industry to a research context, which meant thinking carefully about reproducibility, data provenance, and how to structure pipelines that scientists (not engineers) would need to interact with. It deepened my interest in computational research as a career path.

Hate Speech Detection (NLP)

For my NLP course project, I'm building a hate speech detection system using the Davidson et al. Twitter dataset. The project involves implementing and comparing multiple approaches: a baseline Naive Bayes classifier, an LSTM model, and a fine-tuned BERT model. What makes this project meaningful to me is the real-world complexity — hate speech datasets are noisy, labels are contested, and models trained on them can easily encode existing biases. I'm thinking carefully about evaluation beyond accuracy: false positive rates matter a lot when you're building systems that could silence legitimate speech.
